📊 Phishing Attack Types & Prevention

Learn about different phishing attacks and how to defend yourself

Explore the most common phishing schemes with clear examples, risk ratings, and fast prevention tips. Each card is designed for quick reading so you can understand the threat and act immediately.

🎯 Spear Phishing

HIGH RISK

Targeted phishing attacks aimed at specific individuals or organizations. Attackers research their targets to make the attack more convincing.

Attacker researches target
↓
Creates personalized email
↓
Victim trusts the message
↓
Click malicious link

Prevention Methods:

  • Verify sender email address
  • Don't click links from unknown sources
  • Look for personalization consistency
  • Enable MFA on important accounts

📱 Smishing (SMS Phishing)

HIGH RISK

Phishing through text messages (SMS). Attackers send text messages pretending to be from banks or services you use.

Example:

"Your package delivery failed. Update info: [malicious-link.xyz]"

Red Flags:

  • Unexpected SMS asking for action
  • URL shorteners in SMS
  • Urgent language and time pressure
  • Requests for personal information

Prevention:

  • Don't click SMS links
  • Call the organization directly
  • Never reply with personal info
  • Report suspicious SMS

☎️ Vishing (Voice Phishing)

HIGH RISK

Social engineering through phone calls. Attackers call pretending to be from IT support, banks, or other organizations to trick you.

Example:

"Hi, this is IT support. We detected a security issue on your account. Please verify..."

Red Flags:

  • Unsolicited calls asking for verification
  • Pressure to act immediately
  • Requests for passwords or PINs
  • Phone numbers that look legitimate but aren't

Prevention:

  • Never share passwords over phone
  • Verify by calling the organization back
  • Legitimate IT won't call unsolicited
  • Hang up and call directly

📲 QR Code Phishing

MEDIUM RISK

Malicious QR codes that direct users to phishing websites when scanned. Often used in physical spaces or printed materials.

Example:

QR code on poster directing to fake WiFi login page

Red Flags:

  • QR codes from unknown sources
  • QR codes covering legitimate ones
  • Codes with no context or explanation
  • Suspicious URLs after scanning

Prevention:

  • Check destination before scanning
  • Be wary of QR codes in public
  • Use QR code preview apps
  • Only scan known, trusted codes

✉️ Email Spoofing

HIGH RISK

Forging email headers to make emails appear to come from a trusted source. The email looks like it's from your boss, bank, or trusted organization.

Real sender: attacker@evil.com
Display name: CEO@yourcompany.com

Red Flags:

  • Check actual sender email address
  • Generic greetings
  • Grammar and spelling errors
  • Unusual requests or urgency

Prevention:

  • Always check sender email address
  • Use email authentication (SPF, DKIM)
  • Verify through other channels
  • Enable email filters

💼 Business Email Compromise (BEC)

CRITICAL RISK

Sophisticated attack targeting businesses. Compromises executive email accounts or spoofs them to request money transfers or sensitive information.

Example:

Email from CEO: "Transfer $50,000 to this account for urgent business deal"

Attacker researches company
↓
Compromises/spoofs executive email
↓
Requests money transfer
↓
Millions stolen before detection

Prevention:

  • Require dual approval for transfers
  • Verify requests through other channels
  • Train employees on these tactics
  • Implement email authentication
  • Use MFA on executive accounts
